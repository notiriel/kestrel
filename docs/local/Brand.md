# Kestrel — Brand Strategy

---

## 1. Brand Personality

**1. How would you describe your brand?**

Kestrel is a calm, precise, quietly powerful tool for developers who direct fleets of AI coding agents. It replaces the chaotic desktop of overlapping windows with a panoramic command strip — a single horizontal view where every agent session is visible, every status is readable at a glance, and every permission request surfaces without breaking your flow. It is the falcon's hover: still above the action, seeing everything, intervening only when it matters.

**2. Who are your competitors? (3-5 examples)**

1. **PaperWM** — The original scrolling tiling GNOME extension. Horizontal strip of windows, keyboard-driven. No agent supervision features, no status overlays, no permission integration. A window manager, not a command center.
2. **Niri** — A standalone Wayland scrolling compositor. Similar spatial model but requires abandoning GNOME entirely. No AI agent awareness. Pure tiling WM.
3. **Pop Shell** — GNOME tiling extension from System76. Traditional grid tiling, not scrolling. No agent supervision concept.
4. **Forge / Tiling Assistant** — GNOME tiling extensions. Manual grid layouts. Focused on window arrangement, not workflow oversight.
5. **tmux / Zellij** — Terminal multiplexers. Multiple sessions visible, but confined to terminal. No desktop-level awareness, no window management, no visual status layer.

**3. What sets you apart from the competition?**

Every competitor is a window manager. Kestrel is an **agent supervision environment**. It was designed from the ground up for the emerging workflow where a developer's job is no longer typing code — it is directing, reviewing, and approving the work of multiple AI agents running in parallel. The horizontal strip isn't just a layout preference; it's mission control. Status badges, permission overlays, session-aware workspace naming, and Claude Code hook integration make Kestrel the first desktop tool purpose-built for the human-in-the-loop AI development workflow.

**4. What are the strengths of your brand?**

- **First mover** in the "AI agent supervision desktop" category — no one else is building this
- **Deeply technical credibility** — built as a GNOME Shell extension with hexagonal architecture, immutable domain core, and full test coverage
- **The metaphor is perfect** — a kestrel hovers with effortless precision, scanning everything below, acting only when needed. That is exactly what supervising AI agents feels like
- **Open source** — trust, transparency, community ownership
- **Calm authority** — the brand doesn't shout; it observes

**5. What are some weaknesses?**

- **Niche platform** — GNOME on Linux only; no macOS, no Windows, no KDE
- **Requires Wayland session restarts** to pick up changes — development iteration is slow
- **New category** — "agent supervision environment" doesn't exist yet; users may not know they need it until they try it
- **Name pronunciation** — "Kestrel" is two syllables and well-known in English, but may be less familiar in non-English-speaking developer communities
- **Early stage** — competing against mature, battle-tested tiling extensions with years of user trust

**6. What value do customers get from your brand/product/service?**

You stop losing your agents. When you have five Claude Code sessions running in parallel — coding, asking permissions, waiting for input — Kestrel gives you the view from above. You see every agent's status without switching windows. You approve permissions inline. You maintain spatial memory of where every session lives. The result: you direct more agents, with more confidence, with less cognitive load. Your desktop finally matches the way you actually work now.

## 2. About the Audience

**7. Who are your primary target audience and what do they look like?**

Professional software developers who have adopted AI coding assistants (Claude Code, Copilot Workspace, Cursor, Aider) and are running multiple agent sessions simultaneously. They are Linux users (specifically GNOME/Wayland), comfortable with keyboard-driven workflows, and early adopters of agentic AI development patterns. They are senior/staff engineers or tech leads who spend their day reviewing, directing, and approving AI-generated code rather than writing every line themselves.

**8. Is there a specific gender your brand speaks to?**

No. Kestrel speaks to competence and craft regardless of gender. The brand's tone is professional and inclusive.

**9. Do you have a secondary target audience?**

Power users and Linux enthusiasts who want a better tiling window manager, even without the AI agent workflow. The scrolling-tiling experience stands on its own — it solves the "too many windows" problem for anyone with a complex desktop. They may adopt Kestrel for the tiling and discover the agent features later.

**10. What is it about your brand/product/service that attracts your target audience?**

The recognition moment: "My desktop was designed for single-tasking, but I'm supervising five agents now." Kestrel is the first tool that acknowledges this shift and builds for it. For the tiling-curious: the smooth, spatial, keyboard-driven scrolling model is simply more pleasant than any grid tiler. For the AI-forward: the status badges and permission overlays are a revelation — you didn't know you needed them until you can't live without them.

**11. What are your primary target audiences age group?**

25-45. Developers with enough experience to have adopted AI tools seriously and enough seniority to be directing complex multi-agent workflows. Younger developers experimenting with AI agents. Older developers who value efficiency and have always preferred tiling window managers.

## 4. Brand Tone & Personality

**17. If your brand could speak to your audience directly, how would it introduce itself?**

"You've got five agents working. I'll make sure you can see all of them. You focus on the decisions — I'll handle the view."

**18. How would you describe your brand in three words?**

Calm. Watchful. Precise.

**19. What tone of voice do you use when speaking to your customers?**

Quietly confident. Technical but not jargon-heavy. Concise — Kestrel says what it means in as few words as possible. The tone is that of a senior engineer explaining something clearly: no hype, no exclamation marks, no "revolutionary." Respectful of the user's intelligence. Occasional dry wit.

**20. What metaphors or analogies best describe your brand's personality?**

- A kestrel hovering in still air — effortless precision, total awareness
- An air traffic controller's calm voice during a busy hour
- The bridge of a well-run ship — every station visible, every status known
- A conductor who brings order to complexity with subtle gestures, not force

## 5. Design Preferences

**21. What kind of tone do you want your typography to convey?**

Minimal and precise. A clean geometric sans-serif for the wordmark (think Inter, Geist, or Atkinson Hyperlegible). Monospace for technical contexts (JetBrains Mono). No serif fonts — this is a developer tool, not a luxury brand. The typography should feel engineered: consistent spacing, clear hierarchy, nothing decorative.

**22. Which type of logo do you think would work best for your brand?**

A **logomark + wordmark** combination. The logomark (kestrel silhouette in hover) works standalone at small sizes for the GNOME extension icon, system tray, and favicons. The wordmark ("Kestrel" in the brand typeface) pairs with it for website, documentation, and marketing. The logomark must be instantly recognizable at 16px — this is a system-level tool that lives in your desktop chrome.

## 6. Audiences, Offer & Positioning

**23. What are their top 3 frustrations right now?**

1. **"I can't see all my agents at once."** Alt-Tab between five terminal windows running Claude Code is maddening. By the time you find the one that needs a permission, you've lost context on the others.
2. **"I missed a permission request and my agent was blocked for 10 minutes."** AI agents stop and wait when they need approval. If you don't see the request, everything stalls silently.
3. **"My desktop is chaos."** 15+ windows scattered across virtual desktops. No spatial logic. Every day starts with "where did I put that?" The OS was never designed for this many concurrent contexts.

**24. What product/service are you actually selling?**

A free, open-source GNOME Shell extension that transforms your Linux desktop into a command center for supervising AI coding agents. Horizontal scrolling tiling with agent status badges, inline permission overlays, and Claude Code session integration.

**25. What are their top 3 desired outcomes?**

1. **See everything at once** — all agent sessions visible in a single horizontal strip, status badges showing who's working, who's done, who needs me
2. **Never miss a permission request** — permission cards surface inline, right on the agent's window, impossible to overlook
3. **Spatial peace** — every window has a predictable place; navigation is muscle memory, not a scavenger hunt

**26. Who is this NOT for?**

- Users who don't use Linux/GNOME (no platform support)
- Developers who prefer overlapping/floating window management
- Users who run only one application at a time
- People looking for a complete IDE replacement — Kestrel manages your desktop, not your editor
- Users who want heavy mouse-driven window management (Kestrel is keyboard-first)

**27. What stops them from buying? (Objections)**

- "I'm on macOS/Windows" — hard platform lock to GNOME/Wayland
- "PaperWM already does this" — Kestrel needs to clearly communicate the agent supervision layer as the differentiator
- "I'll just use tmux" — terminal multiplexers handle text; Kestrel handles your entire graphical desktop
- "I don't run multiple AI agents yet" — the tiling experience alone must be compelling enough to adopt; the agent features become the reason to stay
- "Will this crash GNOME Shell?" — extension stability is an existential concern; one bad crash loses trust permanently

**28. What problem do you solve better than anyone else (or differently)?**

No one else has connected "window management" to "AI agent supervision." Every tiling WM treats windows as passive rectangles to arrange. Kestrel treats windows as **live agent sessions to monitor**. The status badges, permission overlays, and hook integration create a feedback loop between your AI tools and your desktop that doesn't exist anywhere else. Kestrel is the first tool that understands your windows aren't just apps — they're autonomous agents that need oversight.

## 7. Practicalities & Success

**29. What does success look like in 3 months + 12 months?**

**3 months:**
- Stable public release on GNOME Extensions with core tiling features working reliably
- Claude Code integration fully functional (status badges, permission overlays, session probing)
- 50+ daily active users who have switched from PaperWM or no tiling
- Zero GNOME Shell crashes in steady-state usage
- Logo and brand identity established; project page live

**12 months:**
- 1,000+ active users; recognized as the default desktop for AI-assisted development on Linux
- Multi-agent support beyond Claude Code (Cursor, Aider, Copilot CLI integration hooks)
- Community contributions: themes, additional agent adapters, workspace templates
- Featured in developer tooling roundups and Linux desktop showcases
- The phrase "Kestrel workflow" is understood in the AI developer community

## 8. Final Thoughts

**30. If your brand could achieve just one thing in the next year, what would it be?**

Establish "Kestrel" as the recognized answer to: "How do you manage your desktop when you're running multiple AI agents?" When someone asks that question on Reddit, HN, or a dev Discord, the answer is Kestrel. Own that category.

**31. Is there anything we didn't cover that you think is important?**

The open-source identity is core to the brand. Kestrel is not a product with a business model — it is a tool built by developers for developers who believe the desktop should evolve alongside AI workflows. The brand should feel community-owned, not corporate. Contributions are welcome. The architecture (hexagonal, tested, documented) is part of the brand promise: this is a serious, well-built tool, not a weekend hack.

**32. Do you have any specific fears, concerns, or hopes for this project?**

- **Fear:** GNOME Shell API instability could break the extension with each GNOME release, creating a maintenance treadmill
- **Fear:** The "agent supervision" framing is too early — most developers aren't running 5 agents yet, and the category may not resonate for another 12-18 months
- **Concern:** The kestrel metaphor must not come across as predatory or aggressive; the brand is about watchfulness and calm, not hunting
- **Hope:** That Kestrel becomes the proof point that the desktop OS needs to evolve for the age of agentic AI — and that this idea spreads beyond Linux

## Additional Prompts

**Non-negotiables + deal-breakers:**

Must include:
- The hovering kestrel posture (wings spread, suspended, scanning) as the core visual identity
- Monochrome viability — the logo must work in single-color on both dark and light GNOME themes
- The word "Kestrel" spelled out — no abbreviations, no clever respellings

Must avoid:
- Aggressive/predatory imagery (talons, diving, catching prey)
- Neon/cyberpunk aesthetics — this is calm and precise, not flashy
- The words "AI" or "agent" in the name or logo — the tool transcends any single AI wave
- Rounded, bubbly, "friendly SaaS" aesthetics — this is a developer's power tool
- Gradients that don't degrade gracefully to monochrome

**Where will the brand be seen most?**

1. **GNOME extension icon** (16-32px) — the most common touchpoint by far; seen every day in the system tray and extension manager
2. **GitHub repository** — README hero, social preview image
3. **GNOME Extensions website** — listing thumbnail and screenshots
4. **Developer blog posts and social media** — shared screenshots, Twitter/Mastodon cards
5. **Conference slides and demo recordings** — Linux desktop and AI tooling talks

**Internal realities:**

- **Team size:** Solo developer + open-source contributors
- **Who will use the assets:** The maintainer for README, website, extension metadata; contributors for docs and presentations
- **Budget:** Zero/minimal — logo and brand assets should be achievable with vector tools (Inkscape, Figma) without commissioning illustration
- **Timelines:** Brand identity needed before public GitHub release
- **Expansion:** If Kestrel succeeds on GNOME, a KDE Plasma port and standalone Wayland compositor version are potential future directions. The brand should not be locked to GNOME visually.
